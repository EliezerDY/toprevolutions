The Revolt

The Dutch Revolt lasted for 80 years from 1568-1648. The northern countries of the Netherlands revolted against the rule of the Spanish Empire under King Phillip II. The united countries became the first republic in Europe in 1588.

Causes for Revolt

The revolt was mainly spurred on by religious differences. Most of the inhabitants of the Netherlands were Protestant, and they were now forced to become subjects of a Catholic monarch who sat on the throne of The Holy Roman Empire. Furthermore, throughout the Middle Ages the Netherlands had enjoyed a large measure of autonomy in appointing its leaders. That clashed with Phillip's approach. He sought to centralize the government, a move that also led to higher taxes.

Leaders of the Rebellion

The Dutch forces united under William I, Prince of Orange known as William the Silent. He had to bring together various factions of the Dutch society, including radical Calvinists who wanted to drive out the Catholic. He also was able to recruit the more passive Catholics to fight against the Spanish for reasons other than religious differences. However, he was assassinated by Balthasar Gerard on July 10, 1584. William was succeeded by his son Maurice who became known as a great military tactician. Maurice passed away in 1625 and was succeeded by his half-brother, Fredrick Henry.

Break in the Action

For 12 years, from 1609-21, there was a cease fire. The armistice was brokered by France and England. The Dutch gained recognition as a European nation while the Spanish gained time to deal with there other internal struggles.

Peace

A peace treaty was fixed on January 30,1648 and signed on May 15. The Netherlands gained full recognition as an independent country free of any foreign rule. However, the southern provinces were still controlled by the Spanish.
